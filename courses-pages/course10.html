Prevention Against Virus Course


Viruses are programs or parts of programs that are usually malicious, loaded onto your computer without your knowledge or permission, and spread by infecting, inserting copies of themselves, and becoming part of other computer programs and files. They are designed to destroy files or disrupt computer operations and can be distributed in various ways, such as through USB devices, emails, internet downloads, and computer networks. Once infected, a computer may experience a variety of issues, including slow performance, system crashes, data loss, and theft of personal information. Viruses rely on the execution of a host program or file to become active and start the infection process. Here are some common characteristics of viruses:




  • Self-executable: Viruses are self-executable programs. They often place their code in the execution path of other programs so that they run after the host program is started.
  • Duplication: They can use the network to multiply, taking advantage of emails or shared resources on local or internet networks.
  • Stealthiness: Many viruses are designed to operate stealthily, without drawing attention from the user or operating system. They can hide in files or hard drives that are not easily detected.
  • Destructiveness: Some viruses are designed to cause damage to data or systems, deleting files, corrupting data, or rendering the system inoperable.

Extension Virótica:


Viral extensions are file extensions associated with files that contain malware, such as viruses, Trojans, ransomware, among others. Hackers often use these extensions to deceive users into opening malicious files, infecting their systems. Some examples of extensions:


  • Executable files (.exe) are executable extensions used in Windows. They can be sent via email or disguised as legitimate programs, such as cracked software versions, to deceive users and compromise the security of their systems.
  • Compressed files (.zip and .rar) are also widely used in attacks. These extensions are applied for data compression, requiring two clicks to view content. Thus, when the file is opened, the machine ends up being contaminated.
  • Office files (.doc, .docx, .xls, .xlsx) can be bait for cybercriminals, who insert malicious macros in these files. Macros are small instructions executed within the file and can be used for malicious activities. It is common to exchange emails within a company with these attachments, making them attractive targets for criminals.
  • PDF files, can contain malicious scripts that are activated when the file is opened. Also, PDF files can contain phishing links that redirect users to fake websites designed to steal personal information or login credentials.

Types of viruses:


  • File viruses: Attach their code to executable files and spread when these files are opened or executed.
  • Boot viruses: Infect the boot sector of disks or storage units and are activated when the system is booted from these devices.
  • Trojan Horses (Trojans): They appear to be legitimate programs but are actually malicious. They can open a port on infected systems so that hackers can access them remotely.
  • Worms: Spread through networks, exploiting vulnerabilities in connected systems, without the need for human interaction to spread.
  • Ransomware: Encrypts files or systems and demands a ransom in exchange for the key to decrypt them.
  • Spyware: Monitors user activities without their knowledge, collecting personal information and transmitting it to third parties.
  • Adware: Displays unwanted ads on infected devices, often causing system slowdown.
  • Keyloggers: Record and transmit the keys typed by the user, allowing hackers to capture confidential information such as passwords and credit card numbers.

Prevention of viruses is essential to protect computers and devices against cyber threats. Here are some recommended practices to avoid virus infections:


  • Antivirus Software: Install good antivirus and antimalware software and keep it updated. Configure it to perform automatic scans regularly.
  • Software Updates: Keep your operating system, web browsers, and other applications updated. Updates often include important security fixes.
  • Emails and Attachments: Be cautious when opening emails from unknown or suspicious senders. Avoid clicking on links or downloading attachments from unsolicited emails.
  • Safe Browsing: Avoid visiting unreliable and suspicious websites. Use a browser extension that can help detect malicious sites.
  • Firewall: Activate your computer's firewall to block unauthorized traffic and protect against network threats.
  • Data Backup: Regularly back up your important data and store it in a secure location. This can help in recovery in case of a virus infection.
  • Security Awareness Training: Conduct regular training sessions to teach employees about different types of cyber threats, such as viruses, phishing, and ransomware.
